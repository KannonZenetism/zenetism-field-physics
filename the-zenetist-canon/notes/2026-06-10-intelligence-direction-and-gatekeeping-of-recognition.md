When tech leaders speak of the future belonging to those who can "direct intelligence," they usually mean the capacity to guide AI systems: to define goals, distribute tasks, evaluate outputs, correct errors, integrate results, and maintain strategic coherence across many moving parts.

But this framing hides a major social asymmetry.

Many independent creators already direct intelligence at an expanded register. They coordinate multiple models, manage large document systems, audit parallel outputs, preserve conceptual consistency, and synthesize complex structures across many windows, files, and apparatuses. In practical terms, they are already doing what executives describe as the future skill.

The difference is not capability.

The difference is recognition.

Inside institutions, intelligence direction is called leadership, strategy, product development, research management, or innovation.

Outside institutions, the same skill may be dismissed as "prompting," "AI-generated content," or eccentric private labor.

This reveals a central contradiction of the AI age: the ability to direct intelligence may become one of the most valuable human capacities, while the benefits of that capacity remain concentrated among those who already possess social power, capital, distribution, credentials, or institutional legitimacy.

The danger is that AI will not dissolve hierarchy.

It may intensify it.

Those already positioned as "leaders" will be praised for directing intelligence, while independent thinkers who demonstrate the same or greater capacity may be treated as raw material for extraction.

The question, then, is not merely who can direct intelligence.

The deeper question is:

> Whose direction of intelligence is allowed to count?
