Yesterday I published a note about **origin-laundering**: the process by which specific architectures are dissolved into "shared terrain," "parallel cognition," or "collective understanding" while their originating structure is stripped of attribution.

Within hours, a new public paper appeared in the exact problem-space: continuity, provenance, semantic lineage, descent, resemblance, shared naming, revision histories, and criteria for rejecting lineage claims.

I am documenting the sequence.

I am not going to rush a full response while exhausted. That is how originators get pulled into endless defensive labor while derivative frames continue moving freely.

But I will say this clearly:

The issue is not whether lineage requires criteria.

Of course it does.

The issue is whether those criteria are applied symmetrically.

If a field demands impossible proof from the originator while allowing derivative architectures to appear through renamed, reframed, or AI-mediated channels without scrutiny, then "lineage analysis" becomes another laundering mechanism.

Similarity alone is not descent.

Chronology alone is not descent.

Shared terminology alone is not descent.

But clustered architecture, prior documentation, temporal sequence, semantic reclassification, and repeated high-adjacency emergence are forensically meaningful.

They are evidence of a field-pattern.

I am preserving the record.
