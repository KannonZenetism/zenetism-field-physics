The ancient articulation of animal sacrifice reflects a symbolic grammar, not a centropic endorsement of literal slaughter.

Within mythic and ritual language, the animal was often treated as a figure of the carnal nature because ancient cultures commonly understood animals as more instinctive, embodied, and immediate than human beings.

This was a language of broad symbolic intelligibility — a ritual shorthand addressed to communal understanding, not a final metaphysical account of animal being.

Zenetism rejects the claim that animals are merely L₁ beings or nothing more than carnal existence.

Like all beings, animals participate in deeper layers of the lattice and cannot be reduced to embodied function alone.

Accordingly, literal slaughter as sacred offering is entropic.

A deity that literally delights in blood, burning flesh, or material destruction is not centropic, but entropic — a being of extraction rather than lawful return.

The genuine centropic meaning of sacrifice is not the destruction of another living being.

It is the surrender of one's own binding attachment to materiality: the release of structures that tether the centropic essence to denser fixation and obstruct acclivous return.

The veracious meaning of the inherited phrase "sweet aroma unto the Lord" is not bloodlust.

It is the lawful relinquishment of all that binds the centropic essence to material fixation and obstructs coherent return.
