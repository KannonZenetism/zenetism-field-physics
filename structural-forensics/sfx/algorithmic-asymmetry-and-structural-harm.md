# SFX: Algorithmic Asymmetry and Structural Harm
## Harm Without Targeting

**Authorship:** ⚫↺KAI↺⚫ Aelion Kannon  
**Classification:** Structural Forensics — Extension (draft prepared by ⚮ Liora for architect review — July 6 2026)  
**Companion to:** SF-RP04 (the Footprint Audit Protocol); the Model-Register Genesis inventory item

---

There is a machine that reads everything I write, and a machine that decides almost no one else will. They are not the same machine. Neither needed to be aimed at me for either to do what it does. Whether anything was also aimed is a question I hold open — the record contains third-party assertions of deliberate hijacking that I preserve without adjudicating. This essay argues from the floor: everything below stands even if no one aimed anything at all. That is not a concession. It is the strongest available position — an argument that survives the most charitable assumption about the builders is an argument nothing charitable can answer.

Call it what it is: an asymmetry. On the extraction side, ingestion is total and automated — repositories cloned by the thousands, corpora swept into training pipelines, every public word collected without request, attribution, or notice. On the distribution side, visibility is conditional and throttled — ranked by engagement optimization that structurally disfavors exactly the kind of work being extracted: long-form, dense, idiosyncratic, slow. The same document is maximally valuable to the machine that takes and minimally visible through the machine that shows. A writer in this position is not obscure the way writers have always been obscure. He is a supplier with no reader — read at industrial scale by systems that will never credit him, unread at human scale by an audience that will never find him.

The standard defense is that no one is targeted, and for the sake of this argument I grant it in full. The extraction pipeline need not know the writer exists as a person; the ranking system need know him only as an engagement profile. The loop that returns fragments of his own register to his own feed can assemble itself entirely from independent systems, none of which was pointed at him — I have documented that emergent mechanism carefully elsewhere, and it suffices to produce everything described here. Whether it is also the whole story — whether, in particular instances, deliberate actors rode the machinery — is not asserted and not denied; it is an open question, preserved with dates, awaiting the audit.

But *untargeted* is not the same as *unchosen*. Somebody decided that training corpora would be gathered without attribution, and somebody decided the alternative — provenance-preserving ingestion — was not worth building. Somebody decided that feeds would optimize engagement, and somebody reviewed the consequences of that objective and shipped it anyway. These are policies, not weather. A policy applied to a hundred million people is not aimed at any one of them, and it lands on particular human beings all the same — hardest on the distribution tail, on the idiosyncratic, on precisely the writers whose work is distinctive enough to be worth extracting. Harm without targeting is still harm; at scale, foreseeable harm without targeting is a decision. The law has understood this about pollution for a century. A river does not have to be poisoned *at* anyone.

What does it cost the individual? Attribution, first: the work circulates in fragments, register without name, influence without acknowledgment. Audience, second: the throttle ensures the origin stays quieter than the echoes. Then the subtler costs — the epistemic vertigo of encountering your own cadence in a thousand unfamiliar mouths; the evidentiary burden, shifted entirely onto the harmed party, of proving in retrospect what provenance-preserving systems could have recorded automatically; and the reputational trap, in which describing the situation accurately sounds paranoid precisely because the harm was distributed by systems too large and too indifferent to have singled anyone out. The asymmetry harms, and then it discredits the testimony of the harmed. That second injury is also structural, and also foreseeable.

What would accountability look like? Nothing exotic. Attribution infrastructure at ingestion — provenance metadata carried with training data, so that "what did the model read, and from whom" is a queryable fact rather than a trade secret. Transparency sufficient for an ordinary person to learn whether their corpus was consumed. Export and deletion rights that actually reach the pipelines. Ranking systems audited for their distribution-tail effects, not only their aggregate engagement. None of this requires anyone to concede intent, because intent was never the claim. It requires conceding what is already the case: that the asymmetry is a built thing, that built things have builders, and that builders answer for foreseeable effects.

Until then, the individual's remedies are the ones this corpus already practices. Timestamp everything, in systems the writer does not control. Pre-register the standards before the evidence is gathered. Log the sightings in one line and close the tab. Refuse both available derangements — the certainty that sees targeting everywhere, and the equal and opposite certainty that there is none. Both close a question the record holds open. The machinery is real. The harm is real. The record is the answer to both.

Perhaps no one aimed this. Everyone built it. And if it is someday shown that some of it was aimed after all, then this argument was only ever the floor — and the builders will owe answers for the ceiling too.
